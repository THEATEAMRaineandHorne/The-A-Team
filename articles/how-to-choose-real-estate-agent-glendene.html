How to Choose a Real Estate Agent in Glendene

Use the same practical questions for every agent you interview so you can compare evidence, campaign strategy, communication, buyer follow-up and negotiation—not just the highest appraisal or lowest fee.

By Edita Andrijasevic · Published 13 May 2026 · Updated 17 July 2026

Choosing an agent is a business decision about how one of your largest assets will be prepared, presented and negotiated. A friendly personality matters, but it is not enough on its own. The most useful comparison is based on what each agent can explain, demonstrate and commit to doing during your campaign.

This guide is deliberately informational. It gives Glendene homeowners a consistent framework for interviewing agents before signing an agency agreement. For direct information about Edita’s selling service, visit the Glendene real estate agent service page.

The quick answer

Choose the agent who gives you the clearest evidence, identifies the likely buyer for your property, explains how the campaign will create competition, and shows a credible process for follow-up and negotiation. Do not rely on one impressive number or one generic presentation.

Start with the purpose of the appraisal

An appraisal should help you understand the property’s likely market position. It is not a guaranteed sale price and it should not be treated as a competition between agents to name the biggest figure.

Ask each agent to separate three things clearly:

  • recent settled sales that are genuinely comparable;
  • current listings that buyers will compare with your home;
  • the recommended pricing or sale method for your particular campaign.

The strongest explanation will normally acknowledge differences in condition, title, land, layout, access, parking, slope, outdoor usability, presentation and location rather than treating every nearby sale as equal.

Use this 10-point agent comparison checklist

Score each agent against the same criteria. Written notes are more reliable than trying to remember three different presentations later.

1. Relevant Glendene evidence

Ask which three or four sales are most useful for understanding your home and why. The agent should explain the similarities and differences rather than simply handing you a long list of suburb results.

2. A clear view of the likely buyer

A first home, family property, investment, unit, cross-lease home and renovation project do not attract exactly the same audience. The agent should be able to describe the likely buyer and the features or concerns that may influence that buyer.

3. Property-specific preparation advice

Good advice distinguishes between work that may improve confidence and spending that is unlikely to be recovered. Ask what should be repaired, cleaned, presented or left alone before photography.

4. A reasoned method-of-sale recommendation

Ask why the agent recommends price by negotiation, deadline sale, auction or another method. The explanation should relate to your property, likely buyer depth, timing and current conditions—not simply the agent’s usual preference.

5. A complete marketing plan

Look beyond “we will put it online.” The proposal should cover photography, copywriting, major portals, database contact, social promotion, signboard, open homes, targeted advertising and the order in which the campaign will launch.

6. A buyer follow-up system

Ask when open-home visitors are contacted, what questions are used to qualify interest and how feedback is reported to you. Useful feedback identifies comparisons, objections, motivation and possible next steps.

7. A communication commitment

Clarify who will call you, how often formal reports are provided and whether you will deal with the person presenting to you or be handed to another team member after listing.

8. A practical negotiation process

Ask for examples of how the agent would respond to a low offer, a conditional offer, two interested buyers or a buyer who says they have reached their limit. You are assessing the thought process, not asking for confidential details about past clients.

9. Openness to conjunctional buyers

Ask whether the agency will work with another licensed agency that has a genuine buyer and what conditions apply. The answer should reflect the seller’s interests and the agency agreement.

10. Transparent fees and campaign costs

Compare commission, administration charges, marketing costs and any optional extras in writing. Also ask what happens to paid advertising if the property sells early or the campaign changes.

Questions to ask every agent

  • Which recent sales are most relevant to my property, and why?
  • Who is the most likely buyer for this home?
  • What concerns could stop that buyer from acting?
  • What work should I complete before photography?
  • Why are you recommending this method of sale?
  • How will the property be promoted beyond the major portals?
  • How and when will buyers be followed up?
  • How frequently will I receive written feedback?
  • How would you handle a low or highly conditional offer?
  • Who will personally manage my campaign?

Red flags to notice during an appraisal

Be cautious when an agent avoids explaining their evidence, dismisses likely buyer objections, pressures you to sign immediately or presents a high price without a plan for how buyers will be encouraged to reach it.

Other warning signs include vague marketing descriptions, no clear follow-up process, inconsistent fee information or promises that depend on buyers behaving exactly as the agent hopes.

How Glendene context changes the comparison

Glendene contains a broad mix of freehold and cross-lease homes, units, family properties, investments and original-condition homes with different access, parking and land characteristics. Buyers may compare these with properties in Glen Eden, Kelston, New Lynn, Henderson and other nearby suburbs.

That does not mean only one “local” agent can sell the property. It means every agent you interview should demonstrate that they understand the relevant buyer comparisons and can explain how your home will be positioned within them.

How to compare two similar proposals

When two agents appear equally capable, compare the detail behind their promises. One proposal may contain a larger advertising package while the other provides more direct agent involvement. One may recommend a short deadline campaign while another proposes price by negotiation.

Return to your priorities: the level of preparation support you need, how much communication you expect, your timing, your comfort with the sale method and the clarity of the negotiation plan.

Make the final decision with a simple framework

  1. Remove any proposal that is not supported by understandable evidence.
  2. Confirm that the recommended campaign suits your property and timeline.
  3. Compare exactly who will manage preparation, buyers and negotiation.
  4. Review all fees and advertising costs in writing.
  5. Choose the agent whose process gives you the greatest confidence—not merely the largest promise.

Frequently asked questions about comparing agents

How many Glendene agents should I interview?

Two or three interviews are usually enough to compare appraisal evidence, communication style, marketing plans and fees without making the process unnecessarily complicated. Ask each agent the same core questions so the comparison is fair.

Should I choose the agent who gives the highest appraisal?

Not automatically. Ask which comparable sales support the figure, what current listings compete with your home and how the proposed strategy is expected to reach that result. A well-explained range is more useful than an unsupported promise.

What should a real estate marketing proposal include?

It should explain the likely buyer group, recommended method of sale, photography and copy, property portals, database and social promotion, open-home plan, reporting, campaign costs and how results will be measured.

How can I assess an agent’s negotiation approach?

Ask the agent to explain how they handle a low first offer, multiple interested buyers, difficult conditions and a buyer who refuses to improve. Look for a clear process rather than a general claim that they are a strong negotiator.

Should commission be the deciding factor when choosing an agent?

Commission matters, but it should be considered alongside the agent’s strategy, communication, marketing, buyer follow-up and negotiation process. A lower fee is not a saving if the campaign receives weaker attention or produces a lower result.
